Intervals & Semitones
An interval is the distance between two notes. Everything in music theory — scales, chords, harmony — is built from intervals. Understanding them gives you the underlying logic of why things sound the way they do.
What is a semitone?+

A semitone (half step) is the smallest interval in Western music — the distance between any two adjacent piano keys including black keys. C to C# is one semitone. E to F is one semitone (no black key between them). There are 12 semitones in an octave.

A tone (whole step) is two semitones. C to D is a tone. Most scale steps are tones with a few semitone exceptions — which gives each scale its character.

On the piano
C → C# = 1 semitone
C → D = 2 semitones (1 tone)
C → E = 4 semitones (2 tones)
C → G = 7 semitones
Named intervals+

Every interval has a name based on how many scale steps it spans and its exact size in semitones.

NameSemitonesSound / feel

Minor 3rd3The minor chord quality. Darker, introspective.
Major 3rd4The major chord quality. Bright, stable.
Perfect 4th5Open, suspended. Strong but not final.

Perfect 5th7Most stable interval after the octave.

Why it matters
When you tap a chord in Key Hub and see the triad notes highlighted, you're seeing stacked 3rds — major or minor — which determine the chord's quality.
The tritone+

The tritone (6 semitones) divides the octave exactly in half. Its two notes want to resolve — either inward or outward by semitone.

In a dominant 7th chord (e.g. G7), the tritone sits between the 3rd (B) and the 7th (F). B wants to resolve up to C, F wants to resolve down to E. This is why V7 → I sounds so inevitable.

G7 tritone resolution → C major
B (3rd of G7) → C (root of C)
F (7th of G7) → E (3rd of C)
Scales & Key Signatures
A scale is a specific sequence of intervals. The pattern of tones and semitones determines the scale's sound. The key signature tells you which notes are sharpened or flattened to maintain that pattern from a given root.
The major scale formula+

Every major scale follows the same pattern of tones (T) and semitones (S):

T–T–S–T–T–T–S

Starting from C: C–D–E–F–G–A–B–C. The semitones fall between degrees 3–4 and 7–8. Any other root requires sharps or flats to preserve this pattern.

Circle of Fifths
Each step clockwise adds one sharp. Each step anticlockwise adds one flat. This is the order Key Hub uses for all 24 keys.
The natural minor scale+

The natural minor scale has its 3rd, 6th, and 7th degrees each one semitone lower than major:

T–S–T–T–S–T–T

A minor: A–B–C–D–E–F–G–A. It uses the same notes as C major but starts from A. Every major key has a relative minor on its 6th degree sharing the same key signature.

Relative pairs
C major ↔ A minor · G major ↔ E minor · F major ↔ D minor
Harmonic minor — why the V chord matters+

In natural minor, the V chord is minor — it lacks the strong pull toward i. Raising the 7th degree by one semitone creates the harmonic minor scale, turning v into a dominant V or V7 with the same powerful resolution as in major keys.

G minor — natural vs harmonic
Natural: G A Bb C D Eb F
Harmonic: G A Bb C D Eb F#

Natural v = Dm (weak pull)
Harmonic V = D / D7 (strong pull → Gm)

9th chords and extensions+

Continue stacking thirds and you get extensions: 9th, 11th, 13th. The 9th is the same pitch class as the 2nd, just an octave higher. Extensions add colour without changing fundamental harmonic function.

In jazz it's common to omit the 5th when adding extensions — it adds little harmonic information. Root, 3rd, 7th, and 9th give the full picture with maximum space.

Gm9 — full vs. practical
Full: G Bb D F A
Practical: G Bb F A (drop the 5th D)
Shell + 9th: LH G F / RH Bb A
Harmony & Function
Harmonic function describes the role a chord plays in relation to the key centre. Every chord has a gravitational pull — toward rest, away from rest, or in between.
Tonic, Subdominant, Dominant+

Tonic (T) — home. The point of rest and resolution. In C major, I (C), vi (Am) and iii (Em) are tonic function — they share notes with I and feel stable.

Dominant (D) — maximum tension, strong pull toward tonic. V7 is the most powerful — the tritone in G7 (B and F) pulls irresistibly to C.

Subdominant (SD) — departure from tonic without dominant's pull. IV and ii. They move harmony away from home gently, often preceding dominant.

The three-function cycle
T → SD → D → T
C → F → G → C
(departure → tension → resolution)
Roman numerals and diatonic chords+

Roman numerals describe a chord's scale position regardless of key. Uppercase = major, lowercase = minor, ° = diminished. The diatonic chord qualities in any major scale always follow:

IiiiiiIVVvivii°
MajminminMajMajmindim

This pattern is identical in every major key. ii–V–I works the same way in C, G, Db — just different note names.

The ii–V–I progression+

The most important progression in jazz. Moves through all three harmonic functions. In C major: Dm7 – G7 – Cmaj7.

The roots move by 4ths (D→G→C) — the strongest root motion in tonal music. The bass does most of the harmonic work just by moving in 4ths.

Practice tip
Learn ii–V–I in all 12 keys. It appears in almost every jazz standard and is the foundation of chord substitution.
Voice leading+

Voice leading is how individual notes move between chords. Good voice leading means each note moves by the smallest interval possible — often just a semitone or tone.

The guide tones (3rd and 7th) are the most important voice leading voices. In a ii–V–I in C: the 7th of Dm7 (C) moves down a semitone to become the 3rd of G7 (B). The 7th of G7 (F) moves down a semitone to become the 3rd of Cmaj7 (E).

Guide tone voice leading: Dm7 → G7 → Cmaj7
3rd: F → F → E (stays, then steps down)
7th: C → B → B (steps down, stays)
Modes
Modes are scales derived from the major scale by starting on a different degree. Each mode has the same notes as its parent major scale but a different tonal centre — and therefore a different character.
The seven modes+
ModeDegreeCharacter

DorianIIMinor with raised 6th. Bittersweet, jazzy.
PhrygianIIIMinor with flattened 2nd. Dark, Spanish.
LydianIVMajor with raised 4th. Dreamy, floating.
MixolydianVMajor with flattened 7th. Bluesy, driving.

How to think about modes practically+

Think of modes as altered major or minor scales — just know what's different.

PhrygianMinor scale with a flattened 2nd

Dorian is the most useful minor mode for jazz — the raised 6th avoids the dark tension of Aeolian and sits naturally over minor 7th chords.

Modal vs. tonal playing+

In tonal playing, you're always moving toward or away from a home chord. Progressions create tension and resolution.

In modal playing, you stay on one chord or drone for a long time and explore the colour of the mode. The tension/resolution cycle is suspended — the sound of the mode itself is the point. Miles Davis's "So What" is the archetypal example: two chords, each held for eight bars — D Dorian and Eb Dorian.

Voicings & Inversions
A voicing is the specific arrangement of notes in a chord — which notes go in which hand, in which order, in which register. Same chord, different voicing = completely different feel.
Inversions+

An inversion is when a note other than the root is the lowest note of the chord.

PositionLowest noteExample (C major)
Root positionRootC E G
1st inversion3rdE G C
2nd inversion5thG C E

Inversions are used for smooth bass movement — if your bass moves by step rather than jumping, the progression sounds more connected.

Close vs. open voicings+

Close voicing: all notes within one octave. Full and direct, but can sound dense in lower registers.

Open voicing: notes spread across more than an octave. More spacious and resonant.

Drop-2: take a close voicing, then drop the second-highest note down an octave. The most common open voicing technique in jazz piano.

Cmaj7 — close vs drop-2
Close (top to bottom): B G E C
Drop-2 (drop G an octave): B E C G
→ LH: C G / RH: E B
Shell voicings+

A shell voicing uses only root, 3rd, and 7th. The 5th is omitted — it adds almost no harmonic information.

Root and 7th go in the left hand (defining the frame), 3rd in the right. Those three notes tell the complete harmonic story. Extensions (9th, 11th, 13th) are then added in the right hand above the shell.

Your voicing
LH: root alone. RH: 7th–9th–3rd–5th. Root in LH is stable and low; all the colour sits in a tight RH cluster above it. This is the "Root + upper structure" voicing in Key Hub.
Quartal voicings+

Instead of stacking thirds, stack fourths. This gives a more ambiguous, modern sound — the voicing doesn't strongly imply a specific chord quality, which creates openness.

McCoy Tyner and Herbie Hancock both use quartal voicings extensively. They work particularly well in modal contexts.

Quartal stack from D (D Dorian)
D – G – C – F (stacked 4ths)
Implies Dm7, but also Cmaj9, Fmaj13... deliberately ambiguous.
Progressions & Movement
A progression is a sequence of chords that creates harmonic motion. The tension and resolution between chords — and the bass movement underneath — determines how it feels.
Root motion by interval+
Root motionFeelExample
Down a 5th / up a 4thStrongest resolution. Feels inevitable.G → C (V → I)
Down a 3rdSmooth, connected. Tonic-function move.C → Am (I → vi)
Up a stepForward momentum without strong resolution.C → Dm (I → ii)
Down a semitoneChromatic tension, dramatic resolution.Db → C
Passing notes between chords+

A passing note is a non-chord tone that fills the stepwise movement between two chord tones. It creates melodic connection between harmony changes.

Diatonic passing notes come from the scale and sound gentle. Chromatic passing notes come from outside the scale and create more tension — they're the backbone of bebop melodic lines.

The most idiomatic use: approach the root or 3rd of the next chord by a semitone from below or above. This is the chromatic approach note.

In Key Hub
The Passing notes shown under each progression show the chromatic or diatonic note between adjacent chord roots. Use them as approach notes in your RH melody.
Cadences+
CadenceMovementFeel
Perfect (authentic)V → IComplete resolution. Full stop.
PlagalIV → I"Amen" cadence. Soft, final.
Imperfectanything → VHalf-close. A question mark.
DeceptiveV → viExpected I, gets vi. Surprise — phrase continues.

The deceptive cadence (V→vi) withholds resolution at the last moment. The vi shares two notes with I, so it partially satisfies while keeping motion going.

Arpeggios and broken chords+

Playing chord tones one at a time adds melodic movement without changing harmony. The classic Alberti bass — root, 5th, 3rd, 5th — creates momentum under a sustained melody.

Jazz arpeggios are less symmetrical. Starting on the 7th or 9th rather than the root creates forward motion — you're already in the colour of the chord and working back to the anchor.

The bebop descent pattern: 9–7–5–3–root is a fundamental jazz lick shape. Descend from a high extension through the chord tones to the root, then use that root as a springboard to the next chord.

Modulation & Pivot Chords
Modulation is moving from one key to another. Done well, it sounds purposeful and surprising at the same time. The pivot chord technique is the smoothest way to move between keys.
What is a pivot chord?+

A pivot chord belongs to both the current key and the destination key. You play it with one meaning, then reinterpret it in the new key and move on. The listener doesn't hear a seam.

C major → G major via pivot
Am = vi in C major (tonic function)
Am = ii in G major (subdominant function)
→ Play Am, follow with D7 → G. The Am reframes retrospectively.
Relative major / minor — the smoothest modulation+

Moving between a major key and its relative minor uses no pivot chord at all — they share the same notes. The shift is purely perceptual: which note feels like home changes, but nothing in the scale changes.

To establish the new tonal centre: land on the new tonic repeatedly, and introduce the harmonic minor raised 7th to create pull toward it. In C major → A minor, the raised G# in E7 signals A minor clearly.

Using Key Hub for modulation+

When you tap a chord and see Also in pills, those are your potential pivot chords. The Roman numeral shown tells you its function in the new key.

The most useful pivots have a different function in each key — tonic in one becoming subdominant or dominant in another creates the strongest sense of motion.

Try it now
Go to C Major → tap the vi chord (Am) → tap "A Minor" in the Also in pills. The Am was vi in C, now it's i in A minor. That's a modulation.
Circle of Fifths modulation+

Moving one step around the circle — to a key one 5th away — is the most natural modulation. The two keys share six of seven notes (only one note changes by a semitone). This is why adjacent keys on the circle feel closely related.

Moving further around the circle introduces more distance and surprise. Moving six steps (tritone key) creates maximum contrast — C major and F# major share only one note.

Jazz standards often make rapid ii–V–I modulations cycling through several keys in quick succession before arriving at the main tonal centre — each ii–V–I is self-contained and resolves cleanly.
