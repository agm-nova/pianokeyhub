Intervals & Semitones
An interval is the distance between two notes. Everything in music theory is built from intervals.
What is a semitone?+

A semitone is the smallest interval in Western music — the distance between any two adjacent piano keys. There are 12 semitones in an octave. A tone is two semitones.

On the piano
C→C# = 1st · C→D = 2nd · C→E = 4st · C→G = 7st
Named intervals+
NameSemitonesFeel
Minor 2nd1Dissonant, tense
Major 2nd2Stepwise motion
Minor 3rd3Minor quality — darker
Major 3rd4Major quality — bright
Perfect 4th5Open, suspended
Tritone6Maximum tension
Perfect 5th7Most stable after octave
Minor 7th10Dominant chord colour
Major 7th11Dreamy, near resolution
Octave12Perfect consonance
The tritone+

6 semitones — divides the octave exactly in half. In G7, the tritone between B and F wants to resolve: B up to C, F down to E. This is why V7→I sounds inevitable.

G7 → C major
B (3rd of G7) → C  ·  F (7th of G7) → E
Scales & Key Signatures
A scale is a specific sequence of intervals. The pattern determines its sound and character.
The major scale formula+
T–T–S–T–T–T–S

C major: C D E F G A B C. Semitones between degrees 3–4 and 7–8. Any other root needs sharps or flats to preserve this pattern.

Circle of Fifths
Each step clockwise adds one sharp. Each step anticlockwise adds one flat.
Natural minor & relative minor+
T–S–T–T–S–T–T

A minor uses the same notes as C major but starts from A. Every major key has a relative minor on its 6th degree sharing the same key signature.

Harmonic minor — why the V chord matters+

In natural minor, v is minor — weak pull to i. Raising the 7th degree by a semitone creates harmonic minor, making V dominant (V7) with strong resolution.

G minor
Natural: G A Bb C D Eb F
Harmonic: G A Bb C D Eb F#
V = D7 → strong pull to Gm

Extensions: 9ths and beyond+

The 9th is the same pitch class as the 2nd, an octave higher. In jazz, omit the 5th — root, 3rd, 7th, 9th tell the full story with maximum space.

Gm9 practical voicing
LH: G F (root + 7th) · RH: Bb A (3rd + 9th)
Harmony & Function
Harmonic function describes the role a chord plays in relation to the key centre.
Tonic, Subdominant, Dominant+

Tonic (T) — home. Rest and resolution. I, vi, iii in major.

Dominant (D) — maximum tension, strong pull toward tonic. V and V7.

Subdominant (SD) — gentle departure from tonic. IV and ii.

Three-function cycle
T → SD → D → T  =  C → F → G → C
The ii–V–I+

The most important progression in jazz. In C: Dm7 – G7 – Cmaj7. Roots move by 4ths (D→G→C) — the strongest root motion in tonal music.

Practice tip
Learn ii–V–I in all 12 keys. It appears in almost every jazz standard.
Voice leading+

Each note moves by the smallest interval possible. The guide tones (3rd and 7th) are key. In Dm7→G7→Cmaj7: 7th of Dm7 (C) steps to 3rd of G7 (B); 7th of G7 (F) steps to 3rd of Cmaj7 (E).

Modes
Modes are scales derived from the major scale by starting on a different degree. Same notes, different tonal centre, different character.
The seven modes+

Modal vs. tonal playing+

Tonal: always moving toward or away from home. Tension and resolution.
Modal: stay on one chord and explore the colour of the mode. Miles Davis's "So What" — D Dorian and Eb Dorian, eight bars each.

Voicings & Inversions
Same chord, different voicing = completely different feel.
Inversions+
PositionLowest noteExample (C)
Root positionRootC E G
1st inversion3rdE G C
2nd inversion5thG C E

Use inversions for smooth bass movement — bass by step sounds more connected.

Shell voicings+

Root, 3rd, 7th only. The 5th is omitted. Root and 7th in LH define the frame; 3rd in RH determines quality. Extensions sit above.

Your voicing
LH: root alone. RH: 7th–9th–3rd–5th. All colour in a tight RH cluster above a grounded LH root.
Drop-2 and quartal voicings+

Drop-2: drop the second-highest note of a close voicing down an octave. Opens the sound.
Quartal: stack fourths instead of thirds. Ambiguous, modern — McCoy Tyner, Herbie Hancock.

Progressions & Movement
Tension, resolution, and bass movement determine how a progression feels.
Root motion by interval+
MotionFeelExample
Down a 5thStrongest resolutionG → C
Down a 3rdSmooth, tonic-functionC → Am
Up a stepForward momentumC → Dm
Down a semitoneChromatic tensionDb → C
Passing notes+

Non-chord tones that fill stepwise movement between chords. Diatonic = gentle; Chromatic = tension and bebop. The chromatic approach: approach the root or 3rd of the next chord by a semitone.

Modulation & Pivot Chords
Moving from one key to another. Done well, it sounds purposeful and surprising at the same time.
What is a pivot chord?+

A chord that belongs to both keys. You play it with one meaning, reinterpret it in the new key, and move on. The listener hears no seam.

C major → G major
Am = vi in C major (tonic)
Am = ii in G major (subdominant)
Play Am → D7 → G. Done.
Using Key Hub for modulation+

Tap a chord and see Also in pills — those are your pivot chords. The Roman numeral tells you its function in the new key. Tap to jump there.

Try it
C Major → tap Am → tap "A Minor" pill. Am was vi, now it's i. That's a modulation.
