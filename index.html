Intervals & Semitones
An interval is the distance between two notes. Everything in music theory — scales, chords, harmony — is built from intervals. Understanding them gives you the underlying logic of why things sound the way they do.
What is a semitone?+

A semitone (also called a half step) is the smallest interval in Western music — the distance between any two adjacent keys on a piano, including black keys. C to C# is one semitone. E to F is one semitone (no black key between them). There are 12 semitones in an octave.

A tone (whole step) is two semitones. C to D is a tone. Most scale steps are tones, with a few semitone exceptions — which is what gives each scale its character.

On the piano
C → C# = 1 semitone
C → D = 2 semitones (1 tone)
C → E = 4 semitones (2 tones)
C → G = 7 semitones
Named intervals+

Every interval has a name based on how many scale steps it spans and its exact size in semitones.

NameSemitonesSound / feel
Minor 2nd1Dissonant, tense. The semitone clash.
Major 2nd2Adjacent scale notes. Stepwise motion.
Minor 3rd3The minor chord quality. Darker, introspective.
Major 3rd4The major chord quality. Bright, stable.
Perfect 4th5Open, suspended. Strong but not final.
Tritone6Extremely tense. The dominant 7th's defining interval.
Perfect 5th7The most stable interval after the octave. Power and openness.
Minor 6th8Bittersweet, emotional.
Major 6th9Sweet, singable. The 9th chord's backbone.
Minor 7th10Dominant chord colour. Jazz and blues.
Major 7th11Dreamy tension, one semitone from resolution.
Octave12Same note, higher register. Perfect consonance.
Why it matters
When you tap a chord in Key Hub and see the triad notes highlighted, you're seeing stacked 3rds — major or minor — which determine the chord's quality.
The tritone+

The tritone (augmented 4th / diminished 5th, 6 semitones) is the most harmonically active interval. It divides the octave exactly in half. Its two notes want to resolve — either inward (both moving by semitone toward each other) or outward.

In a dominant 7th chord (e.g. G7), the tritone sits between the 3rd (B) and the 7th (F). B wants to resolve up to C, F wants to resolve down to E. This is why V7 → I sounds so inevitable — the tritone is pulling the harmony home.

G7 tritone resolution → C major
B (3rd of G7) → C (root of C)
F (7th of G7) → E (3rd of C)
Scales & Key Signatures
A scale is a specific sequence of intervals. The pattern of tones and semitones determines the scale's sound. The key signature tells you which notes are sharpened or flattened to maintain that pattern from a given root.
The major scale formula+

Every major scale follows the same pattern of tones (T) and semitones (S):

T–T–S–T–T–T–S

Starting from C: C–D–E–F–G–A–B–C. The semitones fall between degrees 3–4 and 7–8 (E–F and B–C). This is why C major uses only white keys — E–F and B–C happen to be adjacent white keys on the piano.

Any other starting note requires sharps or flats to preserve this pattern. G major needs F# to maintain the T–T–S–T–T–T–S shape from G.

Circle of Fifths
Each step clockwise around the circle adds one sharp. Each step anticlockwise adds one flat. This is the pattern Key Hub uses to order all 24 keys.
The natural minor scale+

The natural minor scale (Aeolian mode) has a different interval pattern — its 3rd, 6th, and 7th degrees are each one semitone lower than in major:

T–S–T–T–S–T–T

A natural minor: A–B–C–D–E–F–G–A. Note it uses the same notes as C major — just starting from A. Every major key has a relative minor starting on its 6th degree that shares the same key signature.

Relative pairs (same notes, different tonal centre)
C major ↔ A minor
G major ↔ E minor
F major ↔ D minor
Harmonic minor — why the V chord matters+

In natural minor, the V chord (built on the 5th degree) is a minor chord — it lacks the strong pull toward the i that makes V→I so satisfying. To fix this, composers raise the 7th degree of the scale by one semitone, creating the harmonic minor scale.

This raised 7th turns the v minor chord into a V major (or V7) — a dominant chord that creates the same powerful resolution as in major keys.

G minor — natural vs harmonic
Natural: G A Bb C D Eb F
Harmonic: G A Bb C D Eb F#

Natural v = Dm (weak pull)
Harmonic V = D major / D7 (strong pull → Gm)
In Key Hub
Every minor key's v chord shows a purple flag with the harmonic minor alternative. This is the D7 you'd use to create a strong cadence in G minor.
Chord Construction
Chords are built by stacking intervals — specifically thirds — on top of a root note. The exact type of third (major or minor, 4 or 3 semitones) at each position determines the chord's quality.
Triads: the four types+

A triad has three notes: a root, a 3rd, and a 5th. The quality depends on whether those intervals are major or minor:

TypeStructureExample
MajorRoot + major 3rd (4st) + perfect 5th (7st)C E G
MinorRoot + minor 3rd (3st) + perfect 5th (7st)C Eb G
DiminishedRoot + minor 3rd (3st) + diminished 5th (6st)C Eb Gb
AugmentedRoot + major 3rd (4st) + augmented 5th (8st)C E G#

In any major or natural minor key, only major, minor, and diminished triads appear naturally — augmented is rare diatonically.

7th chords+

Add a 4th note — another 3rd above the 5th — and you get a 7th chord. The 7th adds colour and tension. The most important types:

TypeStructureWhere it appears
Major 7th (maj7)Major triad + major 7thI and IV in major
Dominant 7th (7)Major triad + minor 7thV in major; harmonic minor V
Minor 7th (m7)Minor triad + minor 7thii, iii, vi in major; i, iv in minor
Half-dim (m7♭5)Diminished triad + minor 7thvii° in major; ii° in minor

The dominant 7th is the most harmonically active — its tritone (between the 3rd and the minor 7th) creates strong pull toward resolution.

In Key Hub
Toggle "+ 7ths" on the chord map to see the 7th note for every chord in the key. Tap a chord and use the 7th button in the panel to see the full voicing and arpeggio options.
9th chords and extensions+

Continue stacking thirds and you get extensions: 9th, 11th, 13th. These numbers are just the scale degree above 7 — the 9th is the same note as the 2nd, just an octave higher. Extensions add colour and sophistication without changing the fundamental harmonic function.

In jazz and modern piano playing, it's common to omit the 5th when adding extensions — it adds little harmonic information. The root, 3rd, 7th, and 9th give you the full picture of the chord with maximum space.

Gm9 — full vs. practical
Full: G Bb D F A (5 notes)
Practical: G Bb F A (drop the 5th D)
Shell + 9th: LH G F / RH Bb A
Harmony & Function
Harmonic function describes the role a chord plays in relation to the key centre. Every chord has a gravitational pull — toward rest, away from rest, or somewhere in between. Understanding function tells you why progressions feel the way they do.
Tonic, Subdominant, Dominant+

Tonic (T) — home. The point of rest and resolution. In C major, the I chord (C) is tonic. Also vi (Am) and iii (Em) — they share notes with I and feel stable.

Dominant (D) — maximum tension, strong pull toward tonic. The V chord contains the tritone that wants to resolve. V7 is the most powerful dominant — the tritone in G7 (B and F) pulls irresistibly to C major.

Subdominant (SD) — departure from tonic without the strong pull of dominant. IV and ii. They move the harmony away from home gently, often preceding a dominant chord. Think of it as the "away" function — not quite tension, just motion.

The three-function cycle
T → SD → D → T
C → F → G → C
(departure → tension → resolution)
Roman numerals and diatonic chords+

Roman numerals describe a chord's position in a scale regardless of key. Uppercase (I, IV, V) = major chord. Lowercase (ii, iii, vi) = minor. ° = diminished.

The diatonic chords of any major scale always follow the same quality pattern:

IiiiiiIVVvivii°
MmmMMmdim

This pattern is the same in C major, G major, Db major — every major key. The Roman numeral system means "ii–V–I" works the same way in every key, just with different note names.

The ii–V–I progression+

The most important progression in jazz. It moves through all three harmonic functions in four bars (or two, or one). ii (subdominant) → V (dominant) → I (tonic).

In C major: Dm7 – G7 – Cmaj7. The Dm7 prepares the G7. The G7 contains the tritone (B–F) that resolves into the Cmaj7. Each chord is pulling toward the next.

The reason it's so satisfying: the roots move by 4ths (D→G→C), which is the strongest root motion in tonal music. The bass is doing most of the harmonic work just by moving in 4ths.

Practice tip
Learn ii–V–I in all 12 keys. It appears in almost every jazz standard and is the foundation of chord substitution and reharmonisation.
Voice leading+

Voice leading is how individual notes move between chords. Good voice leading means each note moves by the smallest interval possible — often just a semitone or tone — rather than jumping around. This creates smooth, connected-sounding progressions.

The guide tones (3rd and 7th) are the most important voice leading voices. In a ii–V–I in C: the 7th of Dm7 (C) moves down a semitone to become the 3rd of G7 (B). The 7th of G7 (F) moves down a semitone to become the 3rd of Cmaj7 (E). That's smooth voice leading — each chord hands off to the next with minimal motion.

Guide tone voice leading: Dm7 → G7 → Cmaj7
3rd: F → F → E (stays, then steps down)
7th: C → B → B (steps down, stays)
Modes
Modes are scales derived from the major scale by starting on a different degree. Each mode has the same notes as its parent major scale but a different tonal centre — and therefore a different character.
The seven modes of the major scale+

IonianIBright, resolved. The major scale itself.C D E F G A B
DorianIIMinor with raised 6th. Bittersweet, jazzy.D E F G A B C

How to think about modes practically+

The most useful framing: think of modes as altered major or minor scales. You don't need to memorise each as its own interval pattern — just know what's different from major or minor.

ModeThink of it as...
DorianMinor scale with a raised 6th (natural 6th)
PhrygianMinor scale with a flattened 2nd
LydianMajor scale with a raised 4th (#4)
MixolydianMajor scale with a flattened 7th (b7)

Dorian is the most useful minor mode for jazz — the raised 6th avoids the dark tension of Aeolian and sits naturally over minor 7th chords. Most jazz minor-key playing is Dorian-inflected.

Modal playing vs. tonal playing+

In tonal playing, you're always moving toward or away from a home chord (the tonic). Progressions create tension and resolution. Most classical and popular music is tonal.

In modal playing, you stay on one chord (or a drone) for a long time and explore the colour of the mode. The tension/resolution cycle is suspended — instead, the sound of the mode itself is the point. Miles Davis's "So What" is the archetypal example: two chords, each held for eight bars, with D Dorian and Eb Dorian.

Recognising modal progressions — like i–VII–i or a repeated i–VII–VI vamp — tells you to think about the mode rather than reaching for ii–V–I resolution patterns.

Voicings & Inversions
A voicing is the specific arrangement of notes in a chord — which notes go in which hand, in which order, in which register. Same chord, different voicing = completely different feel. This is one of the biggest areas of craft in piano playing.
Inversions+

An inversion is when a note other than the root is the lowest note of the chord.

PositionLowest noteExample (C major)
Root positionRootC E G
1st inversion3rdE G C
2nd inversion5thG C E

Inversions are used for smooth bass movement — if your bass note moves by step rather than jumping, the progression sounds more connected. They're also used to avoid the tonic in the bass when you want the harmony to feel unresolved.

Close vs. open voicings+

Close voicing: all notes within one octave, as tightly packed as possible. Full and direct, but can sound dense in the lower register.

Open voicing: notes spread across more than an octave. More spacious, resonant. Works better in a lower register and in ensemble contexts where other instruments are filling the middle of the sound.

Drop-2: take a close voicing, then drop the second-highest note down an octave. This is the most common open voicing technique in jazz piano. It creates a natural, balanced spread across the hands.

Cmaj7 — close vs drop-2
Close (top to bottom): B G E C
Drop-2 (drop G an octave): B E C G
→ LH: C G / RH: E B
Shell voicings+

A shell voicing uses only the most essential notes: root, 3rd, and 7th. The 5th is omitted — it adds almost no harmonic information and takes up valuable space.

The root and 7th go in the left hand (defining the chord's frame), and the 3rd sits in the right hand. The 3rd determines major vs. minor quality; the 7th determines major 7th vs. dominant 7th vs. minor 7th. Those three notes tell the complete harmonic story.

Shell voicings are the foundation of jazz piano comping. Once you have them, you add extensions (9th, 11th, 13th) in the right hand above the shell — which is exactly what the "Your voicing" pattern in Key Hub does.

The Aidan voicing
LH: root alone. RH: 7th–9th–3rd–5th. This is a rootless upper structure with the 7th at the base of the RH cluster. It works because the root in LH is stable and low, while all the colour sits in a tight RH cluster above it.
Quartal voicings+

Instead of stacking thirds (which gives you triads and 7th chords), stack fourths. This gives a more ambiguous, modern sound — the voicing doesn't strongly imply a specific chord quality, which creates openness.

McCoy Tyner and Herbie Hancock both use quartal voicings extensively. They work particularly well in modal contexts where you want the texture of a scale without committing to a specific chord.

Quartal stack from D (D Dorian)
D – G – C – F (stacked 4ths)
Implies Dm7, but also Cmaj9, also Fmaj13... deliberately ambiguous.
Progressions & Movement
A progression is a sequence of chords that creates a sense of harmonic motion. The tension and resolution between chords — and the bass movement underneath them — determines how a progression feels.
Root motion by interval+

How the bass moves between chord roots has a huge effect on momentum:

Root motionFeelExample
Down a 5th / up a 4thStrongest resolution. Feels inevitable.G → C (V → I)
Down a 3rdSmooth, connected. Tonic-function move.C → Am (I → vi)
Up a stepForward momentum without strong resolution.C → Dm (I → ii)
Down a semitoneChromatic tension, dramatic resolution.Db → C (bII → I)

The cycle of 5ths (moving down in 5ths: C–F–Bb–Eb…) produces the strongest harmonic pull. The ii–V–I does this: D→G→C is two consecutive 5th-descents.

Passing notes between chords+

A passing note is a non-chord tone that fills in the stepwise movement between two chord tones. It creates melodic connection and smoothness between harmony changes.

Diatonic passing notes come from the scale and sound gentle. Chromatic passing notes come from outside the scale and create more tension — they're the backbone of bebop melodic lines.

The most idiomatic use: approach the root or 3rd of the next chord by a semitone from below or above. This is the chromatic approach — one of the most immediately useful melodic devices at the piano.

Passing from Gm to Cm
G → (F# chromatic approach) → C
Or diatonically: G → F → Eb → C
The chromatic F# creates more tension before landing on C.
In Key Hub
The Passing notes shown under each progression are calculated exactly this way — the chromatic or diatonic note(s) between adjacent chord roots. Use them as approach notes in your RH melody.
Cadences+

A cadence is a harmonic ending — how a phrase or section closes. Four main types:

CadenceMovementFeel
Perfect (authentic)V → IComplete resolution. Full stop.
PlagalIV → I"Amen" cadence. Soft, final.
ImperfectI → V (or anything → V)Half-close. A question mark.
DeceptiveV → viExpected I, gets vi instead. Surprise, continues the phrase.

The deceptive cadence (V→vi) is particularly expressive — it withholds the resolution at the last moment. The vi chord shares two notes with I, so it partially satisfies the expectation while keeping motion going.

Arpeggios and broken chords+

Playing chord tones one at a time (rather than simultaneously) adds melodic movement and rhythmic interest without changing the harmony.

Broken chords (Alberti bass, rolling patterns) create momentum in accompaniment figures. The classic Alberti bass — root, 5th, 3rd, 5th — was used throughout Classical-era piano writing to keep energy under a sustained melody.

Jazz arpeggios are less symmetrical. Starting on the 7th or 9th rather than the root creates forward motion — you're already in the colour of the chord and working back to the anchor, rather than climbing up from it.

The bebop approach: descend from a high extension (9th) through the chord tones to the root, then use that root as a springboard to the next chord. The pattern 9–7–5–3–root is a fundamental jazz lick shape.

Modulation & Pivot Chords
Modulation is moving from one key to another during a piece. Done well, it sounds purposeful and surprising at the same time. The pivot chord technique is the smoothest way to move between keys.
What is a pivot chord?+

A pivot chord is a chord that belongs to both the current key and the destination key. You play it with one meaning, then reinterpret it in the new key, and move on. The listener doesn't hear a seam — the harmony just arrives somewhere new.

The trick is that the pivot chord is heard in context of the old key right up until you follow it with a chord that only makes sense in the new key. Retrospectively, the listener reframes the pivot as belonging to the destination.

C major → G major via pivot
In C major: I – IV – ii – V...
The ii chord (Dm) is also vi in F major... but more usefully:
Am (vi in C) = ii in G major
→ Play Am, then follow with D7 → G. The Am was vi, then it becomes ii.
Relative major / minor — the smoothest modulation+

Moving between a major key and its relative minor (or vice versa) uses no pivot chord at all — they share the same notes. The shift is purely perceptual: which note feels like home changes, but nothing in the scale changes.

To make the listener feel the new tonal centre, emphasise the new tonic and dominant. In C major → A minor: land on Am repeatedly, and introduce the harmonic minor E7 to create pull toward Am. The raised G# in E7 signals A minor clearly.

Using Key Hub for modulation+

When you tap a chord in Key Hub and see the Also in pills, those are your potential pivot chords. Each pill shows the chord's Roman numeral in that other key — which tells you its function there.

The most useful pivots are chords that have a different function in each key. A tonic chord in one key becoming a subdominant or dominant in another creates the strongest sense of motion.

Pivot chordIn C majorIn G majorEffect
Amvi (tonic)ii (subdominant)Smooth — relaxed → preparing resolution
Emiii (tonic)vi (tonic)Subtle — both tonic function, key shift is gentle
GV (dominant)I (tonic)Strong — maximum reframe, V becomes home

The practical workflow: play a progression in your current key, land on a chord that appears in your target key, then tap its pill to jump there and continue from that chord's position in the new key.

Try it now
Go to C Major → tap the vi chord (Am) → look at the "Also in" pills → tap A Minor. You've just modulated. The Am was vi in C, now it's i in A minor.
Circle of Fifths modulation+

Moving one step around the circle — to a key one 5th away — is the most natural modulation. The two keys share six out of seven notes (only one note changes, by one semitone). This is why adjacent keys on the circle feel closely related.

Moving two or three steps around the circle introduces more distance and more surprise. Moving six steps (the furthest point, the tritone key) creates maximum contrast — C major and F# major share only one note.

Jazz standards often make rapid ii–V–I modulations, cycling through several keys in quick succession before arriving at the main tonal centre. This creates a sense of harmonic richness without the listener getting lost — each ii–V–I is self-contained and resolves cleanly.
